Yesterday I posted that the man at the centre of my Madlanga Commission graph had just turned state witness. The reply I kept getting was the obvious one: so what do you ask him?

This is where the technology earns its keep.

The graph does not store answers. It stores uncertainty. Every unresolved claim is a pocket of it, and in information terms that is entropy. Most questions barely move it. Ask something the record already settles and you learn nothing. The valuable questions sit at choke points, where one truthful answer collapses a whole cluster of claims at once.

Matlala is the choke point. He is wired into more of the record than anyone outside the people running the inquiry. So the graph can rank, purely from who said what about whom, the questions that would move the most, the ones that only he is positioned to answer.

Four that the public record raises and never closes:

- A ministerial letter of 31 December 2024 directing the disestablishment of the Political Killings Task Team was forwarded to him. Why did a private businessman receive it, and who sent it? (Day 12)
- Testimony alleges senior officers helped his company secure the R360-million SAPS health tender. Which officers, and what did they receive in return? (Day 23)
- He was allegedly told, or said, that "my person will be National Commissioner." Who is "my person"?
- Confidential SAPS material, including officers' ID numbers and an internal docket, was allegedly shared with him while he was under investigation. What was it used for? (Days 48 to 49)

None of this is a finding. It is the shape of the record: high-uncertainty claims sitting inside one man's direct knowledge. He has now agreed to talk.

The graph holds 124 hearing days and roughly 54,000 sworn claims, and for any witness it can tell me exactly which questions are worth the breath. That is the difference between a search box and an evidence engine.

(Matlala's guilty plea is a matter of court record. Everything here concerning other people is untested allegation, not a finding of fact. These are questions, not accusations.)
